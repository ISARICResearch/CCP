ISARIC Clinical Characterisation Protocol Global Toolkit

The Clinical Characterisation Protocol (CCP) is designed for any severe or potentially severe acute infection of public health interest. It is a standardised protocol that enables data and biological samples to be collected rapidly in a globally-harmonised manner.

ISARIC has partnered with global experts to adapt its Clinical Epidemiology Platform, enabling research on the clinical characteristics of emerging infectious disease outbreaks to guide patient care and inform health policy. Dedicated to enhancing evidence generation, ISARIC provides tools and support for research teams to quickly set up studies address the research questions in their context and inform clinical trial design. The platform is also available for collaborative analyses that can characterise disease outbreaks across different variants, countries, and over time.

The ISARIC-WHO Clinical Characterisation Protocol and the ISARIC Clinical Epidemiology Platform is a suite of openly-available, standardised tools to support your research and facilitate global collaboration. Tools within this platform will help you to create a Case Report Form (CRF), launch a database, and perform statistical analysis on the data you collect.The resources are free to use. ISARIC provides support to implement them and supports researchers to retain control of the data and samples they collect.

Steps for operationalisation of CCP

The ISARIC-WHO Clinical Characterisation Protocol (CCP) provides a standardised method for the rapid, coordinated clinical investigation of emerging health threats to inform clinical care, public health response, and clinical trial design. This protocol can be tailored to fit any context. Researchers may add additional research questions, customise the procedures to their sites, and alter the investigations according to their priorities and available resources.

This page provides an overview of the main steps for implementation the CCP and operationalise our tools.It is important that the study is not seen as a separate activity in the facility’s response strategy to the infection of interest.

A Principal Investigator must be appointed. They will be responsible for all aspects of the study, following the main steps outlined below.

We ask that ISARIC is acknowledged and informed if you implement the protocol. Ethical approval of the protocol and all necessary operational and financial arrangements are the responsibility of the investigators.

Inclusion and exclusion criteria

Inclusion criteria

  • Confirmed infection with an emerging or high-consequence pathogen, or
  • Suspected infection with an emerging or high-consequence pathogen, or
  • Confirmed exposure to a pathogen, toxin, chemical or other exposure of public health interest, or
  • Suspected exposure to a pathogen, toxin, chemical or other exposure of public health interest, or
  • Suspected or confirmed exposure to a pathogen, toxin, chemical or other exposure agreed with the investigators and local research teams for the purpose of testing CCP preparedness infrastructure in a pilot study for a maximum period of one week.

Exclusion criteria

For modules 1 and above (where consent is required), refusal to participate by patient, parent or appropriate representative.

Sampling

In some circumstances, robust analysis requires methodical selection of patients for data collection. This is usually the case where the primary aim is large-scale data collection to explore the features of, and risk factors for, illness. Each site has its own requirements, abilities, and limitations to participant recruitment and should select the sampling approach that fits best.

Type of sampling

The sampling method refers to how participants are selected for a study , aiming to ensure representativeness and minimise bias in the analysis.Each site has its own requirements, abilities, and limitations to participant recruitment and should select the sampling approach that fits best.

  • Ad hoc - Screen all patient as you are able without systematic selection
  • Census - Screen all potential participants who present to your setting.
  • Census for a defined population - Screen all patients admitted to ICU.
  • Sequential/systematic sampling - Screen patients based on their time of presentation. E.g., every 3rd patient who presents to the hospital, or all patients who present on even calendar days (2nd, 4th, 6th)
  • Simple random sampling - Use a tool to randomly determine if each patient is screened. E.g., use an online tool to generate a random list of Yes/No variables in the desired proportion and apply them sequentially as patients present (note: this should be done so that no one knows the next variable)
  • Opportunistic - No defined or consistent criteria. Patients are screened as and when staff have time available (note: this is not ideal, but sometimes necessary).

Prepare the Case Report Form

ISARIC and partners have developed a ARChetype standardised case report form (CRF) for mpox, H5Nx, Covid and Dengue.

The form includes the variables needed to efficiently characterise the natural history of the disease, regions, time and populations. It is structured to quickly identify risk factors for severe outcomes, understand the mechanisms of disease, and provide key evidence to inform the design of clinical trials.

    Statistical Analysis Plan

    An ISARIC Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP) has been developed to align with the CCP and ISARIC ARchetype CRFs. This can be tailored to your needs and used for data analysis.

    Visualisation and analysis tools

    ISARIC also provides a set of analytic tools called VERTEX, designed to perform the analyses outlined in the SAP using ISARIC's data structures. VERTEX generates tables and visualisations using Python code, and displays this information for each relevant research question or study endpoint, in a web application dashboard. VERTEX is built using reproducible analytical pipelines. It is designed to allow ISARIC partners and the broader scientific community to develop, contribute to, and share their own analytic codes so that other researchers can use and reproduce them.
